Day 1 — Immediate (within 24 hours)

The clock starts the moment your employment ends

▾
You have exactly 60 calendar days. Not business days. Calendar days. The grace period begins the day your employment terminates, not when you find out.
  • Call your immigration attorney immediately. If you don't have one, get one today. Murthy Law, Fragomen, and Berry Appleman all handle urgent H-1B matters. Cost: $300–$500/hour. Worth every dollar.
  • Confirm your exact grace period end date. Write it on paper and put it on your wall. If laid off February 1, the grace period ends April 1.
  • Do NOT cash out your 401(k). Early withdrawal = income tax + 10% penalty + 30% non-resident withholding if you leave the US. On $200K: you could lose $100K+. Leave it. Always.
  • Review your vested RSU shares. Vested RSUs are ordinary shares deposited in your brokerage account — they belong to you outright and there is no deadline to sell them. You can hold them indefinitely. The decision to sell or hold is purely financial, not a deadline. Note: stock options (ISOs/NSOs) are different — those have post-termination exercise windows. Confirm which type you hold.
  • Review your severance agreement before signing. Specifically: does it affect COBRA eligibility timing? Does it include a non-compete that limits your job search? Do not sign under time pressure without reviewing.
  • Assess your AC-21 status. Has your I-485 been pending for more than 180 days? If yes, you have job portability — this is a fundamentally different situation. If no, you are in a time-critical scenario.
2

Days 1–7 — Financial Stabilization

Protect your family's financial security while the job search runs

▾
  • Elect COBRA health insurance. You have 60 days from loss of coverage to elect. Cost: $600–$1,800/month for a family. Expensive, but non-negotiable. Medical emergency without coverage is catastrophic.
  • Calculate your runway. Monthly expenses (mortgage/rent + utilities + food + insurance + kids' activities). Divide savings by monthly burn. That number is how many months you have before a financial crisis. Know it precisely.
  • Do NOT stop mortgage payments. If struggling, call your lender immediately for a forbearance — they have hardship programs. But do not miss a payment silently; it damages credit and triggers a cascade.
  • Pause all non-essential automatic withdrawals. Subscriptions, memberships, investment auto-contributions. Preserve cash. You can restart everything once you have an offer.
  • Check your spouse's H-4 EAD status. If your spouse has an approved H-4 EAD, they can work immediately. If not yet approved, apply now — though processing takes 3–6 months.
  • Notify your bank of your situation. If you have a mortgage, many banks have hardship programs. Understanding your options before you need them is always better than discovering them under pressure.

Days 1–30 — Immigration Options (pursue all simultaneously)

The immigration decision tree — know your options before the clock runs out

▾
The goal is to secure a new H-1B sponsor before Day 60. Pursue all options simultaneously, not sequentially.
  • Option A: H-1B transfer to new employer (BEST OUTCOME). The new employer files an H-1B transfer petition. Premium processing ($2,805) guarantees a decision in 15 business days. This is priority #1. Contact 3–5 recruiters on Day 1.
  • Option B: H-4 status (if spouse is on H-1B). Valid immigration status, but no work authorization until H-4 EAD is approved (3–6 months). Requires your spouse's H-1B to be stable and their employer supportive. File the H-4 application immediately as a protective measure, even while pursuing Option A.
  • Option C: B-2 extension (last resort). Buys up to 6 months. You CANNOT work on B-2 — not even informally, not for a friend's startup, not as a consultant. Violation terminates your ability to adjust status in the US. Use only if actively pursuing Option A.
  • Option D: O-1 (extraordinary ability). If you have significant publications, patents, awards, or industry recognition, you may qualify. Requires 1–3 months and $5–10K in attorney fees. Investigate immediately in parallel — it won't be fast enough alone but may be the answer.
  • Do NOT travel internationally. Once you leave the US, re-entry requires a valid H-1B visa stamp. If your visa stamp is expired, you cannot re-enter without getting a new stamp at a US consulate abroad — a process that can take weeks to months.
  • If I-485 is pending >180 days: you have EAD. If your EAD card has been approved, you can work for ANY employer immediately. You are not truly in a 60-day crisis — you have job flexibility. Still consult an attorney to understand status maintenance.

The 5 Financial Decisions You Must Get Right

These mistakes cost immigrants tens of thousands — or everything

▾
Decision 1: Never touch the 401(k).
I'll say it again. The math on early withdrawal for an H-1B holder who might leave the US: income tax (37%) + 10% penalty + potential 30% NRA withholding on top = you could receive as little as 20 cents on the dollar. A $300,000 401(k) could net you $60,000. Leave it. Borrow if desperate. Do not cash out.
Decision 2: Decide what to do with your vested RSU shares.
Vested RSUs are shares you already own outright — there is no post-termination deadline to sell them. They sit in your brokerage account and can be held indefinitely. The real decision is whether to hold concentrated single-stock exposure during a period of financial uncertainty, or sell and diversify into cash. In most layoff situations, selling to build liquidity is the right financial move — but it is your choice, not a deadline.
Decision 3: Do not accept a below-market offer in panic.
The 60-day clock creates pressure that employers know about. Some will offer below-market compensation to a visibly desperate candidate. Know your market rate. Premium processing ($2,805) is worth paying to give yourself 15 more business days of negotiating time.
Decision 4: The PERM restart calculation.
If your I-485 has NOT been filed, changing employers means the new employer must file a new PERM. Your priority date (from the I-140) is preserved if your old I-140 is not revoked. Ask: Is my old employer likely to revoke my I-140? Contact an attorney before assuming anything.
Decision 5: If you had prior good planning, this is manageable.
6-month emergency fund, no house bought before I-485, FBAR filed, RSU shares tracked — if you built these foundations, a layoff is stressful but not catastrophic. If you haven't, the next job is to build them. That's what financial planning is for.
Where you are in the immigration timeline determines everything.
A layoff 3 years into your H-1B career is a completely different event than a layoff with your I-485 pending for 6 months. The difference is the difference between a financial crisis and a minor inconvenience. Knowing your exact status is the foundation of all planning.
